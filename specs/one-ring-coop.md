# the one-ring co-op

> everyone is a member. smbs and cells. one cooperative to rule them all.

## what is destroysaas?

destroysaas is a cooperative. not a platform, not a startup, not a marketplace — a cooperative. businesses and product teams are all members of the same organization, with equal say in what gets built, who builds it, and how the money works.

it's structured as a **Limited Cooperative Association (LCA)** — a real legal entity where every member has voting rights, ownership, and enforceable contracts.

## who are the members?

there are two kinds of members. they have **equal voting rights**. both pay **monthly dues** to the cooperative.

**businesses** — companies that need software. they propose ideas, pledge money toward specific projects, and vote on what gets built and who builds it.

**cells** — small product teams (typically cooperatives themselves) that design, build, and maintain software. they bid on projects, submit monthly budgets for their work, and vote alongside businesses on cooperative decisions.

that's it. no investor class. no platform class. no special privileges. one member, one vote. both classes pay dues. both classes have skin in the game.

## how a project gets selected

any member can propose an idea. here's how it goes from idea to funded project:

### step 1: propose and pledge

a business submits an idea — "we need a shared invoicing tool" — and pledges what they'd pay monthly. other businesses with the same problem pile on with their own pledges.

when total monthly pledges hit the **funding threshold** (e.g., $1,000/month), the idea becomes a candidate project.

### step 2: the cooperative votes on the project

all members — businesses *and* cells — vote on whether to green-light the project. this is a cooperative-wide decision because treasury funds are shared. every funded project affects the whole cooperative's budget.

simple majority. one member, one vote. businesses and cells vote as equals.

the board can fast-track routine approvals (projects under a certain budget, unanimous pledge support, etc.) to avoid voting fatigue. contested or large projects go to a full vote.

### step 3: cells bid on the work

once a project is approved, certified cells that want to build it submit proposals — what they'd build, how they'd build it, and what they'd charge monthly.

**the bid must fit the budget.** a cell's proposed monthly cost cannot exceed the project's pledged monthly revenue. the bid establishes a **monthly cap** — the maximum the cell can draw each month. if no cell can do the work within budget, the project goes back to the pledge phase to attract more funding — or the scope is reduced.

not every cell will bid. some won't have the capability or interest. cells must be certified members in good standing to bid (see "cell certification" below).

### step 4: the cooperative votes on the cell

the **project's pledging businesses** and the **board** vote to select which cell gets the contract. this keeps cell selection focused on the people with direct stake in the project, rather than requiring every member to review every technical proposal.

the winning cell gets a contract to build and maintain the software.

## how money works

the financial model is intentionally simple.

### money in

- **monthly dues (all members)** — every member pays dues. businesses and cells alike. this funds the cooperative's operating costs: legal fees, insurance, hosting, platform maintenance, board expenses, and the reserve fund.
- **project pledges (businesses)** — on top of dues, businesses pledge toward specific projects. pledges are ring-fenced — money pledged for the invoicing tool goes to the invoicing tool, not to general operations.

### money out: monthly budgets

cells submit a **monthly budget** for the coming month's work, drawing against the monthly cap established in their bid. this is a forward-looking estimate — the cell predicts what they'll need and asks for it in advance. in a good month, they may draw less than the cap.

here's how it works:

1. **monthly budget, submitted ahead of time.** at the start of each month, the cell submits a budget request for the coming month's work. this includes projected labor, hosting costs, tools, and any other expenses.

2. **the budget is public.** every member of the cooperative can review every budget. there are no secret budgets, no hidden costs.

3. **the board reviews and approves.** the elected board reviews budgets as part of routine operations. they can flag anomalies and request justification before payment. this is the practical check — you don't need every member to audit every line item.

4. **the cooperative pays the budget.** once approved, the budget is paid from the project's ring-fenced fund.

5. **cells that are efficient keep the difference.** if a cell budgets 100 hours and finishes in 80, they keep the margin. this is by design — it rewards efficiency. budgets are self-reported to some degree, and that's acceptable because transparency keeps it honest.

6. **budget overruns.** if a cell underestimates and needs more, they submit a revised budget request. the board approves overruns up to a cap (e.g., 20% of the original budget). anything beyond that requires a vote of the project's pledging businesses. the cell bears the risk of bad estimates — this is the incentive to estimate honestly.

### emergency and maintenance work

monthly budgets cover planned work. for emergencies (server outages, security vulnerabilities, critical bugs):

- each active project includes an **on-call retainer** as a line item in the cell's monthly budget. this covers the cell's availability for unplanned work.
- emergency expenses beyond the retainer are reported in the next month's budget with a written explanation. the board reviews these as part of normal budget approval.

### what prevents abuse?

- **public budgets.** every budget is visible to every member. sunlight is the disinfectant.
- **board review.** the elected board reviews budgets monthly and can reject or request revision before payment.
- **the vote.** if a cell is consistently overcharging or underdelivering, any member can call a vote to replace them. the cell keeps its membership but loses the contract.
- **competition.** other cells can bid to take over a project if the current cell isn't delivering value.
- **budget-pledge alignment.** a cell's monthly budget cannot exceed the project's monthly pledge revenue. the money in caps the money out.
- **pledge churn protection.** if businesses cancel pledges and a project's revenue drops below the cell's monthly cap, the cell's budget is automatically scaled down to match available revenue. if revenue drops below a minimum viability threshold, the board can pause the project. cells accept this risk as part of the contract — it's the same risk any business faces when customers leave.
- **IP indemnification.** cells warrant that their work is original or properly licensed. if a cell introduces IP violations, they indemnify the cooperative.

## cell certification

not every team can be a cell. before a cell can bid on projects, they must be admitted as a member:

1. **apply.** the cell submits an application covering their team, portfolio, technical capabilities, and cooperative structure.
2. **board review.** the board evaluates the application against baseline standards: security practices, code quality, availability, and cooperative values.
3. **member vote.** all members vote on whether to admit the cell. this ensures the full cooperative has a say in who joins.
4. **dues.** once admitted, the cell pays monthly dues like any other member.

certification can be revoked by member vote if a cell consistently fails to meet standards.

## dispute resolution

disagreements happen. when a business says the work is unfinished and the cell says it's done:

1. **the board mediates first.** the board reviews the deliverables against the cell's proposal and budget. they make a ruling.
2. **either party can appeal to the membership.** if the board's ruling is unsatisfactory, the dispute goes to a full member vote.
3. **the contract is the backstop.** every cell contract includes clear deliverables, timelines, and acceptance criteria. if it's in the contract, it's enforceable.

## how patronage works

the cooperative tracks **patronage** — how much each member has contributed over time. this is standard cooperative practice (IRC Subchapter T) and matters for two reasons:

1. **surplus distribution.** if the cooperative runs a surplus at the end of the year, it gets distributed back to members in proportion to their patronage — but through **separate pools** (see below).

2. **historical record.** patronage is how the cooperative remembers who built it. early members who took the risk get credit for that risk in perpetuity.

### what counts as patronage?

for **businesses**: dues and project pledges. you contributed money — that's your patronage.

for **cells**: dues plus the budgets the cooperative *paid* for their work. patronage is based on what the cooperative actually spent, not what the cell requested. this avoids the perverse incentive of inflating budgets to increase patronage.

### surplus distribution: separate pools

the year-end surplus is split into two pools before distribution:

- **50% to the business pool** — distributed pro-rata to businesses based on their total dues + pledges for the year.
- **50% to the cell pool** — distributed pro-rata to cells based on their total dues + paid budgets for the year.

this prevents highly-paid cells from absorbing surplus that was generated by business contributions, and vice versa. each class benefits from the value they created.

the 50/50 split is a starting point. the membership can vote to adjust it.

patronage is tracked automatically by the platform. no manual accounting.

## governance

### one member, one vote

every member gets one vote on cooperative-wide decisions, regardless of how much they've pledged or invoiced. a $50/month business has the same vote as a $500/month business. a two-person cell has the same vote as a ten-person cell.

this is the cooperative principle. patronage determines surplus distribution. voting is equal.

### what gets voted on

| decision | who decides |
|----------|-----------|
| approve a new project | board (routine) or all members (contested/large) |
| select a cell for a project | project's pledging businesses + board |
| replace a cell on a project | all members |
| accept a new business member | board (routine) |
| accept a new cell member | all members |
| approve budget overruns > 20% | project's pledging businesses |
| change dues or fee structure | all members, supermajority |
| change bylaws | all members, supermajority |
| elect board of directors | all members |
| allocate surplus | all members |

### the board

the cooperative elects a board of directors from its membership. the board handles day-to-day operations: reviewing monthly budgets, approving routine new business members, fast-tracking uncontested projects, mediating disputes, and managing the cooperative's operating expenses. the board is elected annually. any member can run. the board reports monthly to the full membership — transparent by default.

## what the cooperative owns

- **all code** — every line written by every cell is owned by the cooperative. cells do work-for-hire.
- **all data** — member data belongs to the cooperative, held in trust for its members.
- **the brand** — destroysaas, the domain, the identity.
- **the infrastructure** — hosting, CI/CD, the platform itself.

## fork freedom

all code is open-source. any member can leave at any time and take the code with them. you can fork the code — you can't fork the membership, the brand, or the treasury.

this is the safety valve. if the cooperative ever stops serving its members, the members can walk. the code goes with them.

**what you get as a member that you don't get from just forking:** hosted infrastructure, ongoing maintenance, governance rights, community support, new features, and the cooperative's brand and trust. the code is free. the service is the membership.

## how it starts

1. **the founder files the LCA.** one person does the paperwork. colorado. articles of organization, bylaws, EIN, bank account.
2. **first members join.** businesses and cells sign up, pay dues, and ratify the bylaws.
3. **first project.** a business proposes an idea, others pledge, the cooperative votes to approve it, certified cells bid within the pledge budget, the pledging businesses and board select a cell.
4. **first budget.** the cell submits a monthly budget. the board reviews it. members can inspect it. the cooperative pays it.
5. **it repeats.** more projects, more cells, more members. the model scales because the rules are simple.

## why this works

- **alignment.** businesses and cells are peers, not adversaries. they're co-owners of the same organization, voting on the same decisions. both pay dues. both have skin in the game.
- **simplicity.** two member types. equal votes. monthly budgets. public finances. that's the whole model.
- **transparency.** every budget, every vote, every dollar is visible to every member.
- **incentives.** businesses get software they own. cells get steady contract work. efficient cells earn more. the cooperative distributes surplus to everyone.
- **accountability.** budgets must fit pledge revenue. the board reviews every dollar. any member can call a vote. cells compete for contracts.
- **resilience.** if a cell underperforms, replace them. if the cooperative underperforms, fork the code. nobody is trapped.
